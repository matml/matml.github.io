Exponential growth

1.1 Exponential growth

Suppose we have one variable, N⁢(t)N(t), that represents the size of a population as a function of time. We can describe how N⁢(t)N(t) changes with time by means of an ordinary differential equation (ODE) of the form

d⁢Nd⁢t=“birth”−“death”.\frac{{\rm d}N}{{\rm d}t}=\text{``birth''}-\text{``death''}. (1.1)

One of the first people to try this was Robert Malthus, about 1798, who considered the world’s human population with the ODE

d⁢N⁢(t)d⁢t=b⁢N⁢(t)−d⁢N⁢(t)=(b−d)⁢N⁢(t),\frac{{\rm d}N(t)}{{\rm d}t}=bN(t)-dN(t)=(b-d)N(t), (1.2)

where bb and dd are positive constants. The solution of the equation is

N⁢(t)=N⁢(0)⁢e(b−d)⁢t.N(t)=N(0){\rm e}^{(b-d)t}. (1.3)

The population will grow exponentially, if b>db>d, or decrease exponentially, if b<db<d.

In the nineteenth century, based on simple ideas of this type, some people concluded that high birth rates in a population would inevitably cause a huge increase in population followed by catastrophic famine. The model is too simple to draw accurate conclusions about real populations, but the concept of exponential growth (or decay) is important.
